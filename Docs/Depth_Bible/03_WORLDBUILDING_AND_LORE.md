## 1.0 The Pitch in One Paragraph

Hearthbound Hollow takes place in **Embershade Vale**, a small autumnal river-valley in a temperate northern climate, in a world that resembles late-19th-century rural Europe but with a single — *and only one* — supernatural element: **memory is physical**. It can be removed, held, traded, polished, restored, and consumed. Memory is the only magic. There are no spells, monsters, dragons, gods (though there is religion), curses, or potions. This single-departure-from-realism is the discipline that gives the world its quiet, lived-in feel.

## 2.0 The Cosmology of Memory

### 2.1 The Three Laws of Memory (the world's only metaphysical commitments)

> **Law 1 — Memory has weight.**
> Memories are not abstract. They exist as a substance the world calls *vyr* (rhymes with *fear*). When a memory is removed from a person, the vyr leaves their body and becomes — through a process not understood, not even by Keepers — a glass orb.

> **Law 2 — Memory is divisible but not duplicable.**
> A memory can be split into facets (Severing). Two memories can be Woven into one. But no memory can be copied. If Doris sells her wedding day, she has sold it. She remembers that she was married. She does not remember the wedding.

> **Law 3 — Memory remembers itself.**
> A memory orb, even broken, retains its essential structure. A polished orb returns the memory more vividly than it was *lived.* This is why people sell.

These three laws are **never spoken aloud by the game.** They are the underlying rules an attentive player infers. The Keeper's Code (§ 4) is the in-world articulation; the laws above are the metaphysics under it.

### 2.2 What memories are made of (the vyr glossary)

The substance *vyr* has properties the player will learn through experience:

| Property | Sensation | Game effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Density** | A heavy orb settles. A light orb floats slightly. | Weight slot on shelves. |
| **Temperature** | Warm orbs = joy / grace. Cold orbs = grief / dread. Lukewarm = composite. | Bee response (Codex 04); buyer preference. |
| **Clarity** | Glass-clear to amber-fogged. | Polishing minigame target. |
| **Cracks** | Hairline to web. | Cleansing minigame target. |
| **Hum** | Faint audible note when held still. Pitch = palette. | The audio designer's signature element (Codex 14). |
| **Aftertaste** | When a person consumes a memory (which is possible), it leaves a taste. | Pickle's tasting glossary; Memory Sommelier path (Codex 16). |

Vyr is not "magic energy." It is not consumed by mages. It is not a battery. **It is the chemistry of being a person**, given a name.

### 3.2 The hidden seventh location — *The City of Wells* (only ever heard of, never visited)

A growing industrial city three days' travel east. The City has electricity. The City has trains. The City has banks. The City is where the Memory Stock Exchange is being built. **The City never appears in-game.** It is the rumored "outside" that justifies the village's quietness and the villain's existence.

A producer might be tempted to add the City as DLC. *We strongly recommend against this.* The City's power is in being unseen. (See Codex 16 § 6 for the DLC roadmap that keeps the City off-camera.)

## 4.0 The Keeper's Code

The Hollow's craft is governed by an unwritten code, codified into seven vows that a Keeper takes (the first time you sit at the workbench in Chapter 1, the predecessor's Echo Hologram recites the seven). The vows are *the moral spine of the game* and every choice can be traced back to one of them.

### 4.1 The Seven Vows

1. **The Vow of Consent.** *No memory shall be taken from a person who has not, in their right mind, given it.* (Foundational. Breaking this is the game's only true sin. There is a hidden "thief" path that violates this — see Codex 09 § 6.)

2. **The Vow of Return.** *A memory found is to be returned if its owner can be found.* (You may decline to return; this has consequences but is not "evil." There are reasons.)

3. **The Vow of the Whole.** *Cleanse with care. A cleansed memory must still be the memory. A memory cleansed to silence is a memory killed.* (The cleansing minigame's risk-management — Codex 13.)

4. **The Vow of the Quiet Glass.** *A memory in your keeping is not for telling. The Hollow's shelves are not gossip.* (You may break this. The consequence is the village's slow trust-loss.)

5. **The Vow of the Honest Coin.** *Pay what the memory is worth, not what the seller will accept.* (Anti-exploitation moral. You may exploit. You will be known.)

6. **The Vow of the Open Door.** *No villager shall be turned away from the Hollow because they cannot pay.* (Free service for the poor. Becomes the basis of the Confession Booth — Codex 06 § 6.)

7. **The Vow of the Last Light.** *When you cannot help, you may sit with them. To sit with someone is to do something.* (The vow that means listening is mechanically valid — you can choose Listen with no transaction and still earn trust.)

### 4.2 Vow tracking

Each vow has a hidden integrity meter (0–100). Breaking a vow drops the integrity. At low integrity:

- Vow 1 < 50 → the Echo Hologram refuses to teach you the Severing technique.
- Vow 2 < 50 → Ms. Inkwell stops sharing letters with you.
- Vow 4 < 30 → villagers stop selling their *deep* memories. You hit a soft narrative wall.
- All vows < 50 → the Predecessor's Echo Hologram speaks her grief. (Set-piece scene.)

Vow integrity is *recoverable* but not easily. This is the moral consequence engine that makes the game's choices *matter* without ever blocking the player.

## 5.0 The Predecessor — *Marin Vell* — The Keeper Before You

> ⚠ Lore spoiler-rich section. This is the canon.

### 5.1 Identity

**Marin Vell.** Age 47 at the time of her disappearance, three years before the game opens. Held the Hollow for 22 years. The most accomplished Keeper in three generations.

She did not die. She did not run away. She did not leave the village. **She *severed herself* into 17 pieces of memory and dispersed them.** The pieces are with various villagers, hidden in various places, and (critically) **inside the Hollow itself**, as the Echo Hologram speaks from them.

### 5.2 Why she did it

Three reasons, revealed over the game's arc:

1. **She was being asked to violate Vow 1.** A delegation from the City of Wells came to her with a contract from the nascent Memory Stock Exchange: harvest the village's memories at scale, on consent forms the village did not understand. She refused. They threatened her, then the village.

2. **She had a memory she could not keep and could not sell.** What it was is the game's deepest secret. It is revealed in Revelation Chapter 24. *It is the predecessor's memory of doing harm — once, decades ago, to a person who later became Old Mariska.* (See § 6.)

3. **She believed dispersal was an act of love.** The Hollow needed a successor. The village needed protection. If she dispersed, the City could not extract her. If the village reassembled her — *if you do, player, by collecting all 17 pieces* — she would return.

### 5.3 Reassembly — the endgame

Across the game, the player can find or be given 17 memory fragments. Each is keyed `MAR-001` through `MAR-017`. Some are gifts. Some are bought. Some are stolen. Some require Severing operations on other memories. Some are bee-honey deposits (Codex 04 § 5). Some require players to break the Code (see Codex 08 § 7 for the ethics).

If the player assembles all 17 — possible in a single playthrough, but the natural single-playthrough rate is ~6 → encouraging multiple playthroughs and Letter-Bird co-op (Codex 15) — the player can choose:

- **Reassemble Marin** (she returns; she takes the Hollow; you choose your next chapter — see Codex 16 endings).
- **Honor her choice** (do not reassemble; she remains dispersed; you become the next dispersed Keeper, if you choose).
- **Keep her** (refuse to reassemble; keep all 17 fragments on your shelf as a private collection — a darker ending, foreshadowed by Old Mariska as a moral mistake).

This is the game's most expensive narrative moment. It is hand-written in three full versions.

## 6.0 The Forgotten Year — The Game's Largest Set-Piece

### 6.1 What it is

In **1989** (the in-world year; the game's "present" is 1992), Embershade Vale collectively forgot something. *Every villager*. The forgetting was caused by a memory event the village does not have words for. The cleric thinks it was divine intervention. Mariska knows what it was. The Mayor is afraid to ask.

### 6.2 What actually happened

A child — **the boy Ren's older sister, Mira** — died in a river accident. The village's grief was so profound and so collective that, in a single autumn evening, the village's Keeper (Marin's predecessor, *Old Cas*, never seen) gathered the entire village's memory of Mira into a single shared orb and buried it under the schoolhouse. The village agreed. The village forgot they had agreed.

Three years later, Marin found the orb. She did not unbury it. She left a note.

Three years after *that*, Marin dispersed herself. The note is part of fragment MAR-009.

Three years after that, **you arrive.**

### 6.3 How the player encounters it

- **Day ~30** (first autumn): Ren visits the shop. He cannot tell you anything specific, but he says he misses someone. He cannot name them. *(The Boy Who Knows He's Missing Something — Revelation Chapter 11.)*

- **Days ~30–80**: pattern-echoes around "the river," "the schoolhouse," "the autumn of '89" begin appearing in older villagers' memories. The player triangulates.

- **Day ~80–110**: the player can find the orb under the schoolhouse (a one-time exploration set-piece with no combat — see Codex 13 § 5 for the *Search* puzzle structure).

- **Day ~110+**: The player must decide what to do with the orb.
  - **Open it.** The whole village remembers Mira. Some are grateful. Some — including Mira's mother, Halia (yes, *that* Halia, Codex 02 § 4) — are not. *Halia's character arc inverts: she has been Halia-without-a-daughter for three years and has built a life on that. Restoring it breaks her.*
  - **Leave it.** The village stays in the Forgetting. Halia stays unbroken. Ren stays without a sister. *Pickle the cat will know.* This is also a valid choice.
  - **Share it as fragments.** Distribute the memory among willing villagers. Each carries a part. Mira is partially restored. Halia keeps her stability. Ren learns her name. (The compromise ending.)

This single mid-game set-piece carries the **highest emotional weight in the game**. It is the cozy-with-real-consequence proof. It is what we will show in the vertical-slice playtest (Codex 16 § 9).

## 7.0 Religion, Folklore, and Calendar

### 7.1 The folkloric religion of Embershade Vale

A syncretic, Bronze-Age-flavored religion practiced loosely by everyone except Brother Anselm (who practices it intensely but doubtfully). The pantheon is minor; the rituals are major. Three "Quiet Hands" (the harvest, the river, the hearth) are addressed in small daily offerings. There is no holy book. There is no priesthood beyond Anselm. There is folklore — and the folklore matters more than the doctrine.

**Why this matters mechanically:** the religion gives the village its festivals, holidays, and rhythms (Codex 16 § 2 — Seasonal Calendar). It also provides Brother Anselm's character arc (Codex 02 § 4).

### 7.2 The Folkloric Bestiary — *creatures of the world that aren't creatures*

There are no monsters. There are *folkloric figures*:

- **The Lampman.** A man villagers say walks the road at dusk lighting lamps. Has never been seen by two villagers at once. Probably real, in some sense.
- **The Eel-Wife.** The river's mistress. Old story. Probably not real.
- **The Singing Hive.** A hive that sings in spring. Doris has heard it once. *Real.* (Codex 04 § 5.)
- **The Forgotten Door.** A door that appears in dreams. Some villagers have walked through it. They cannot say what was on the other side. *Probably the predecessor's Echo Hologram, dream-side.*

These are all *narrative* elements, never combat. They are how the world feels old and large without being filled with monsters.

### 7.3 The Calendar — *Eight Months of Embershade*

The game year has 8 named months (slightly compressed from real-world), 7 days a week, ~10 in-game days per month (~80 days per game-year, ~24–30 in-game weeks for a typical playthrough at one in-game day per ~25 minutes):

| Month | Real-world analogue | Tonal palette |
|---|---|---|
| **Rust-Month** | September | Beginning. Harvest. Honey. |
| **Spire-Month** | October | The classic autumn. The game starts here. |
| **Cinder-Month** | November | First frost. Inwardness. |
| **Long-Night** | December | The festival of the hearth. (Codex 16 § 2.) |
| **Saltlight** | January | Bright cold. Letters. Stillness. |
| **Thaw-Month** | February | The river loosens. Memory restoration is easier (mechanical bonus). |
| **Greenmouth** | March | First grass. The bees return. |
| **Bloomfast** | April | The end of the game year. Festival of returning. |

Each month has 1–2 festivals (Codex 16 § 2) and a tonal weather pattern that affects the Memory Dream lensing (Codex 11).

## 8.0 Antagonist Lore — *The Memory Stock Exchange*

The game has no villain you fight. It has a villain you can *refuse*.

**The Memory Stock Exchange** is a new financial institution in the City of Wells. It claims to "liquidate the emotional reserves of the rural population at fair market value." It deploys agents to outlying villages to gather memory inventory. One such agent visits Embershade in late autumn of the game year.

### 8.1 The agent — *Mr. Vance Ashby*

**Vance Ashby**, 38, dapper, kind-mannered, perfectly polite, dressed in a city suit. He is *not* sinister in person — he genuinely believes he is doing good. He thinks rural memory is being wasted by people who could be using the capital. He thinks the Hollow is a charming relic. He calls Pickle "your charming little assistant."

His arrival is the **Vance Arc** (Codex 08 § 5). The player can:

- Refuse to trade with him entirely (the canonical choice; respected by the village).
- Sell him specific memories (he pays well; some villagers find out; consequences ripple).
- *Trick him* — sell him assembled-procedural junk memories ("compost memories," see Codex 10) at a markup. (The comedic choice; Pickle approves.)
- Befriend him (a late-game route that opens a complex DLC seed — Codex 16 § 6).

Vance is the only character in the game who explicitly tries to commodify memory at scale. **He is what the predecessor refused. He is what the player must also refuse — or be tempted by.** He is also, importantly, *funny* (Codex 07 § 6). The cozy-with-edge balance requires the antagonist to be charming, not menacing.

### 8.2 What the Exchange is, structurally

A satirical mirror of: late-stage gig-economy emotional-labor commodification, the wellness industry, and the rural-extraction patterns of industrialization. The game is **deeply political** about this without ever delivering a monologue. *The politics is in the action.* The choice to refuse Vance, repeatedly, is the politics.

## 9.0 The Hollow's Architecture

### 9.1 Floor plan

A two-storey timber-and-stone building on a slight rise just outside Embershade village center.

**Ground floor:**
- **The Shop** (front). Three shelving units (expandable to 7). Counter. Two chairs. A teapot kept permanently warm.
- **The Workbench Room** (back). The workbench. Storage. The garden door.
- **The Garden** (back yard, herbs + small apiary).
- **The Pantry & Tea Cabinet** (small, off the workbench room).

**Upper floor:**
- **The Keeper's Quarters** (single room, bed, desk, window).
- **The Library** (a tight room with three walls of books — Marin's library; many books are clue-bearers).
- **The Locked Room** (locked from inside. You find the key in Spire-Month week 3. Inside is the Echo Hologram device — see Codex 12 § 3 — and Marin's seven-vow plaque.)

**The cellar:**
- **The Cold Vault** (where the most fragile memory orbs are stored — climate matters in this world).
- **The Composting Room** (where failed memories are composted into garden soil — Codex 04 § 5).
- **The Sealed Door** (cellar's far wall. Cannot be opened in the base game. *DLC seed.*)

### 9.2 Hollow upgrades

The shop upgrades — adding shelves, expanding the cellar, building the apiary, lighting the upper floor — are a primary progression track (Codex 04 § 2). Upgrades use Coin + *Cinder* (a soft currency earned from helping the village; see Codex 10 § 2).

## 11.0 Things The World Does NOT Have (Lore Discipline)

The world's smallness is the world's charm. **The following must never enter the game.** This is a hard list.

- ❌ Combat of any kind (including humorous "fights").
- ❌ Magic other than memory itself.
- ❌ Gods who appear, speak, or act.
- ❌ Time travel.
- ❌ Multiple worlds / planes / dimensions.
- ❌ Technology beyond ~1880 rural Europe (no electricity in the village; no telephone; the City has these, mentioned only).
- ❌ Romance with the player (cozy buyers report bouncing off romance-systems-by-default in narrative cozy; we opt out).
- ❌ Children in danger as a *consequence of player choice.* (Children may be sad. Children may not be put in mortal peril by your decisions. This is a Pell Doyne firewall — Codex 06.)
- ❌ Animal death as a *plot device.* (Animals exist. Animals age. We do not weaponize their death.)

The disciplines above are the world's contract with the cozy audience.
