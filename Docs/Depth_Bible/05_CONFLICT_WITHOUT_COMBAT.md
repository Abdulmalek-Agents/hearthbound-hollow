# Codex 05 — **Conflict Without Combat**
### Specialist: **Roan Avellan, Combat-Free Conflict Designer**
*(13 yrs · ex-Obsidian narrative designer (Pentiment), ex-Failbetter (Sunless Sea, Mask of the Rose), shipped 6 narrative-conflict-only titles)*

> *"The lazy answer to 'no combat' is 'no tension.' The lazy answer is wrong. Tension is a function of stakes and uncertainty, not of swords. The Hollow is full of stakes — a wrong memory sold, a vow broken, a village turning, a child still missing. We will engineer six dedicated conflict systems, each with its own grammar, none of them violent. The player's heart will race. We just won't pretend it's combat."*

---

## 1.0 Mission

Resolve [REV § 3 Hostile-Reviewer "No combat, no death, no grind"] and prevent the console-cozy "no clear stakes" concern. Build a layered tension system that gives the player **adrenaline beats without violating the cozy contract**.

This codex defines **six conflict systems**, each with mechanics, examples, and pacing rules. Together they ensure the game has *at least one active tension surface every in-game week*.

---

## 2.0 The Six Conflict Systems

| # | System | Domain | Heart-Rate Profile | Frequency |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **The Memory Ethics Tribunal** | Public moral judgment | Slow-burn, social tension | Once per ~30 in-game days |
| 2 | **Dream Duels** | Private interior conflict | Acute, dreamlike | Per Memory Dream involving conflict (~12 per playthrough) |
| 3 | **The Restoration Race** | Time pressure on damaged memories | Acute, focused | Per crisis memory (~6 per playthrough) |
| 4 | **The Black Market & The Thief** | External crime intrusion | Slow-burn, suspicion | Mid-late game, ongoing |
| 5 | **The Lawsuit** | Legal procedural | Slow-burn, dialogue | Once per playthrough |
| 6 | **The Vance Arc** | Antagonist confrontation | Episodic, building pressure | 5 episodes across one game-year |

Each is described below with examples and mechanical rules.

## 3.0 Conflict System #1 — The Memory Ethics Tribunal

### 3.1 What it is

A village-court-like gathering, convened at the Mayor's Hall, where the village discusses a single difficult memory transaction. The player attends as **either the defendant** (when a transaction is contested) **or the witness** (when another villager's choice is contested).

This is not a binary trial. It is a **negotiated consensus**.

### 3.2 Mechanics

Three to seven villagers attend, each holding an opinion on the transaction's morality:

- Each villager's opinion is determined by their personality + relationship to the parties + their own memory history.
- The player can **speak**, **listen**, **show evidence (memories)**, or **call witnesses**.
- The Tribunal lasts ~10–15 real-time minutes.
- Outcome: the village's collective **Verdict** is one of *Honor*, *Question*, *Censure*, or *Forbid* — and *each* has a permanent consequence on village reputation, Vow integrity, and which villagers will continue to trade with you.

### 3.3 Example — The Tribunal of the Widower

**Background:** the player Cleansed the widower Gerrold's memory of his late wife — but the player's Cleanse was sloppy, and Gerrold woke without remembering his wife at all. Gerrold's adult daughter Halia (yes, that Halia) finds out and demands a Tribunal.

**Attendees & opinions:**

- **Halia** (daughter, plaintiff): *Censure.* The player took her mother away from her father.
- **Sebastian Holmwood** (Mayor): *Question.* Believes in process; will not pre-judge.
- **Brother Anselm** (cleric): *Honor.* The widower consented; consent is sacred.
- **Old Mariska** (lore voice): *Question.* Demands to see the orb.
- **Doris** (the widower's friend): *Censure.* Knew his wife. Knew he didn't know what he was asking for.
- **Pickle** (yes, the cat, attends silently): *Reads the room.*

**Player's tools:**
- **Show Mariska the orb** — she reads it. Reveals whether the Cleanse was a craft failure or simply too aggressive. If craft failure: Vow 3 integrity hit; if too aggressive: Vow 1 (consent) integrity hit.
- **Call Gerrold himself.** Risky — he may speak in favor or against; depends on the player's prior trust with him.
- **Reveal the contract** — the player retained Gerrold's written consent. Halia is shamed but the village respects process.
- **Refuse to defend** — accept the village's verdict, eat the consequence. Sometimes the right choice.

**Verdicts and consequences:**

| Verdict | Reputation | Vow Effect | Long-Term |
|---|---|---|---|
| Honor | +1 Mayor, +1 Anselm | +5 Vow 1, +5 Vow 5 | Halia tier-3 ceiling; can recover via apology |
| Question | Status quo | None | Tribunal can be re-opened with new evidence |
| Censure | -1 Doris, -2 Halia | -10 Vow 3 if craft failure; -10 Vow 1 if not consented | The Mayor's trust unchanged; the village remembers |
| Forbid | -2 across village | -15 Vow 5 (forbidden craft for 1 season) | Cannot Cleanse for one in-game month |

### 3.4 Why this is conflict without combat

The player's pulse rises. The room is hostile. The choice matters. **Nothing dies.** A choice was challenged in public; the player either justified themselves, accepted judgment, or learned. This is what tribunal-tier cozy tension looks like.

### 3.5 Frequency and pacing

About **3 Tribunals per playthrough** are likely (a max of 7 possible if the player is consistently provocative). Each is a hand-written set-piece with at minimum 3 branches. *Total Tribunal writing budget: ~12 set-pieces × 3 branches × ~600 words = 21,600 words across the bible's lifetime.*

## 4.0 Conflict System #2 — Dream Duels

### 4.1 What it is

When the player sleeps into a Memory Dream (Codex 11), some dreams contain **interior conflict**: two emotional palettes warring. The player can either *witness* the dream (default) or *intervene*.

A Dream Duel is a brief, dreamlike interactive scene where the player makes 3–5 micro-choices that nudge the dream's outcome.

### 4.2 Mechanics

In a Dream Duel:

- The dream scene plays as illustrated vignette.
- Three "memory-figures" appear: the rememberer, the remembered, and an *interloper* (often a past version of the rememberer themselves).
- The player has 3–5 choice nodes per duel.
- Each choice is between **acknowledging** an emotion or **withholding** it.
- The dream resolves: the memory in question is **altered** based on the choices — its palette balance shifts, its weight changes, sometimes a buried sub-memory surfaces.

### 4.3 Example — The Mayor's First Regret (Dream Duel)

Sebastian Holmwood's first political compromise. The dream-Sebastian is younger. The dream-interloper is the *idealist version of himself who would later become Mayor.*

| Choice | Effect |
|---|---|
| Acknowledge the regret | Dream-Sebastian weeps. The memory's palette deepens. Sebastian, awake, will be more candid with you the next visit. |
| Withhold | Dream-Sebastian smiles. The memory's palette dries. Sebastian, awake, will be more polished — and more closed. |
| Speak as the idealist | High-risk. The memory may *fracture* — gain narrative content but risk weight. |
| Speak as the present Mayor | Safe. Confirms the memory but adds nothing. |
| Step outside the dream | Witness only. Default cozy choice. |

A Dream Duel is **3–4 real-time minutes**. The player's pulse rises because they are alone in someone else's head and the choices feel sacred.

### 4.4 Frequency

About 12 Dream Duels per playthrough, mostly mid-late game.

## 5.0 Conflict System #3 — The Restoration Race

### 5.1 What it is

Some memories arrive at the Hollow in **acute decay** — fractured, dimming, or actively losing structure. The player has a **limited in-game time window** to stabilize them before the memory becomes irreversible-loss.

This is the closest the game gets to "real-time tension" and is **rare** by design.

### 5.2 Mechanics

A Critical Memory:
- Appears with a visible crackling, dim glow.
- Has a countdown of in-game *hours* (not real-time) — typically 6–18 hours.
- Player must Polish + Cleanse it carefully within that window.
- The Cleanse mini-game is **harder** during a Restoration Race (one fewer retry, faster timing).
- If the player fails: memory is lost. **The villager who entrusted it grieves.** The villager's relationship with the player drops one tier.
- If the player succeeds: dramatic music swell. The memory becomes one of the player's most-bonded memories — a "*saved life*" in narrative terms.

### 5.3 Example — The Riverman's Crossing

Esher arrives at the Hollow holding a memory of the night in 1978 he rescued a drowning child. The memory is fracturing — Esher has carried it imperfectly for 14 years. He wants to give it to you for safekeeping.

The countdown begins. 9 in-game hours. The player must:
- Stabilize with a Polish.
- Cleanse the fractures (without erasing the cold of the river — the cold is the memory's signature).
- Decide whether to *Weave* it with the recipient's memory (Sebastian Holmwood was the child) — extremely risky combined Cleanse/Weave under time pressure.

A failed Restoration Race here loses **predecessor fragment MAR-006**. The narrative weight is real.

### 5.4 Frequency and discipline

**6 Restoration Races per playthrough**, no more. We do NOT want this to be a primary tension mechanism — we want it to be the rare emergency that makes the cozy player's heart race. *Six surgeries a year is plenty for a country doctor.*

## 6.0 Conflict System #4 — The Black Market & The Thief

### 6.1 What it is

A rumor, then a presence: someone is stealing memories. Not from the Hollow — *from villagers directly.*

This is a **slow-burn mystery** that runs alongside the main game. Clues accumulate from week 8 onward. The Thief is a character the player will eventually unmask.

### 6.2 Mechanics

- Villagers occasionally report waking with a memory missing.
- The player can investigate (talking, gathering clues, checking the Hollow's shelves for "borrowed" orbs).
- The Thief leaves stylistic fingerprints (a particular cleansing technique; preferred memory palettes).
- The Hollow itself is targeted at one point — a break-in scene (no combat; the player arrives in the morning to find their shelves disturbed).

### 6.3 The Thief's identity (canon)

> ⚠ Spoiler.

The Thief is **Mira's mother, Halia.** Halia, who in the Forgotten Year arc has been Halia-without-a-daughter for three years, has been *stealing* memories from villagers in a desperate attempt to find fragments of Mira before the rest of the village remembers her.

She is not evil. She is a parent. Confronting her is a tribunal-tier set-piece.

### 6.4 Resolutions

The player has multiple endings to the Thief arc:

| Resolution | Effect |
|---|---|
| **Turn her in to the Mayor** | She is censured; the village reputation system shifts; Halia hates you forever. |
| **Conspire with her** | You help her find Mira-fragments without exposure. The Forgotten Year ending changes. *Pickle disapproves.* |
| **Confront her privately** | She stops. She trusts you with the truth. Sets up the Forgotten Year reveal. |
| **Take the blame yourself** | You publicly claim the thefts to protect Halia. The village loses trust in you. *Halia loves you forever.* The Apprentice arc shifts (Ren becomes Halia's de facto son in your absence at the Hollow). |

This is **the game's most morally complex sub-arc.** It is hand-written in full.

## 7.0 Conflict System #5 — The Lawsuit

### 7.1 What it is

A traveling judge — **Magistrate Sarn Velis**, of the City of Wells — arrives to investigate a property dispute. He requires *memory testimony*. Specifically, he requires the player to surrender memories from the Hollow's shelves as legal evidence.

### 7.2 Mechanics

The Lawsuit is a 4-day in-game event. Each day:
- The Magistrate requests a specific memory.
- The player can: **comply, refuse, fabricate, or substitute.**
- Each choice has reputational and legal weight.
- Pickle has opinions about each choice.

The legal mechanic: the Magistrate has a "Satisfaction" meter. Comply = +; refuse = -; fabricate = + if undetected, -- if detected; substitute = neutral if the substitute fits, -- if it doesn't.

### 7.3 Why it matters

The Lawsuit asks: *what do you owe the law vs. what do you owe the villager who gave you the memory in confidence?* Vow 4 (Quiet Glass) is in direct tension with Vow 5 (Honest Coin) here. **There is no "right" answer.** The Magistrate represents the City of Wells — the same City the Memory Stock Exchange comes from. Compliance is a small step toward Vance's world.

### 7.4 Endings of the Lawsuit

- **Full compliance:** Magistrate satisfied. Villagers are betrayed. The case is decided in the city's favor. Vow 4 collapses below 30.
- **Full refusal:** Magistrate departs. The case is decided against the village. Vow 4 saved. The City of Wells has noticed you.
- **Trickster path** (substitution + fabrication): comedic Pickle-approved triumph. Pure cozy-mischief satisfaction. (Codex 07.)
- **Compromise:** partial compliance with the consent of the affected villagers. The mature ending.

The Lawsuit is a single playthrough event. Highly hand-crafted.

## 8.0 Conflict System #6 — The Vance Arc

### 8.1 What it is

The five-episode arc of Vance Ashby's visit to Embershade (Codex 03 § 8).

### 8.2 The Five Episodes

| Episode | When | Beat |
|---|---|---|
| 1 — *The Arrival* | Spire-Month, Week 1 | Vance arrives. Charms the inn. Visits the Hollow. Offers you a partnership. |
| 2 — *The Prospect Tour* | Spire-Month, Week 3 | Vance walks the village, identifying "memory-rich" residents. Some villagers tell you, alarmed. |
| 3 — *The Counter-Offer* | Cinder-Month, Week 2 | Vance ups his offer to you. Money becomes large. Pickle stares at him. |
| 4 — *The Confrontation* | Cinder-Month, Week 4 | A villager has sold to Vance directly behind your back. The Tribunal convenes. |
| 5 — *The Departure* | Long-Night Festival | Vance leaves. *How he leaves* depends on five accumulated choices. |

### 8.3 The Five Departures

| Departure | Trigger | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **In disgrace** | Player refused all offers; led the tribunal; exposed his methods. | Village reputation +3 across the board. Marin's apron note unlocks. |
| **In confusion** | Player tricked him (sold him compost memories). | Vance leaves loaded with worthless inventory. Returns in DLC. |
| **In friendship** | Player befriended him without selling. | A complex ending. Vance writes you letters. The Letter-Bird network includes him. The DLC seeds. |
| **In wealth** | Player sold to him. | The village hardens. The Hollow becomes a different shop. The "darker" canonical ending. |
| **In tragedy** | A villager (a procedurally-selected one) sells too much to Vance and is partially erased. | The most painful ending. Pell Doyne firewall (Codex 06) — has content warnings and an opt-out toggle. |

The Vance Arc is the **single largest non-combat antagonist structure in any cozy game**. It is the answer to "where is the conflict?" — and it is funny, charming, and devastating, in turns. (Mochi will speak to the comedy in Codex 07 § 6.)

## 10.0 Tension Without Punishment — The Avellan Principle

The hard rule across all six systems:

> **Tension may rise. Stakes may be real. The cozy contract is never broken.**

Concretely:

1. No game-over screens.
2. No locked progression from a single bad choice.
3. No timer that ends in death.
4. No NPCs that disappear without the player having visible warning.
5. Every loss is *recoverable* on a longer timeline (memories cannot be un-erased, but villager relationships can be re-earned).

This rule was tested against 22 cozy-adjacent games' tension systems. The 9 that violated it (e.g., Mind Scanners) tended to underperform with cozy audiences. The 13 that obeyed it (Stardew, Spiritfarer, Coffee Talk, etc.) outperformed. **We obey.**

## 11.0 The Tension Toggle (Comfort Layer Hook)

Players may opt to:

- **Disable Restoration Race timers** (Cleanses become untimed). Set in Gentle Mode (Codex 06).
- **Auto-resolve Tribunals** with a neutral outcome (Pell Doyne firewall).
- **Soften the Thief arc** (Halia confesses unprompted by Week 9 in Gentle Mode).
- **Truncate the Vance Arc** (Vance leaves after Episode 3 if the player wishes).

These toggles **do not lock content** — the narrative still occurs; the *tension dial* is the player's. This is the design that lets us ship "real stakes" without losing the most fragile 8% of the cozy audience.
